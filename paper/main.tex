\begin{abstract}
S2 Activation---the standard nonlinearity in SO(3)-equivariant networks---applies a
pointwise activation on the sphere via spherical harmonic (SH) expansion, truncation to
$\ell_{\max}$, and finite quadrature. We present a systematic study decomposing
its errors along two axes: \emph{activation choice} and \emph{quadrature strategy}.
We identify a fundamental \emph{truncation--aliasing dichotomy}: coefficient error is
dominated by truncation (insensitive to oversampling), while equivariance error is dominated
by aliasing (reduced $10$--$450\times$ by $3\times$ oversampling).
We further show that EquiformerV2's default e3nn grid uses \emph{Driscoll--Healy}
equiangular quadrature, and that swapping it for \emph{Gauss--Legendre}---a drop-in
change requiring no retraining---halves the grid points needed for matched equivariance.
Controlled experiments
on EquiformerV2/QM9 (6 activations, 5 seeds each) reveal a surprising
\emph{smoothness--expressiveness tradeoff}: the Spearman correlation between theoretical
equivariance error and downstream MAE is $\rho = -0.94$ ($p = 0.005$)---less equivariant
activations perform \emph{better}. We trace this to Jacobian throughput: the nuclear norm
(total singular-value mass), not effective rank, predicts task performance
($\rho = -0.94$). Analysis of per-layer equivariance error reveals a two-phase pattern---an
$8$--$16\times$ jump from layer~0 to 1, then a plateau---explained by the transition
from indirect to direct cross-degree contamination in the transformer architecture.
\end{abstract}

%========================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%========================================================================

S2 Activation is the standard pointwise nonlinearity in SO(3)-equivariant neural networks,
including SCN~\citep{scn}, eSCN~\citep{escn}, and EquiformerV2~\citep{equiformerv2}.
Given spherical harmonic (SH) coefficients $\{c_{\ell m}\}$ for
$\ell = 0,\dots,\ell_{\max}$, the operator projects onto the sphere, applies a scalar
nonlinearity $\sigma$, and integrates back via quadrature. Both the finite bandwidth
$\ell_{\max}$ and the finite quadrature introduce error and can break SO(3) equivariance.

Prior work has studied specific quadrature schemes for S2 Activation~\citep{e3nn} and
observed that equivariance degrades with non-smooth activations, but a systematic
decomposition of the error sources has been lacking. In this paper we:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Show that \textbf{spectral leakage} (energy generated above $\ell_{\max}$) is
    strongly correlated with equivariance error across activations (\S\ref{sec:activation}).
  \item Identify a \textbf{truncation--aliasing dichotomy}: coefficient error is
    truncation-dominated (sampling-insensitive), while equivariance error is
    aliasing-dominated (sampling-sensitive) (\S\ref{sec:sampling}).
  \item Introduce a \textbf{Jacobian isotropy} metric that measures how uniformly
    S2 Activation explores output directions, and show it is orthogonal to leakage
    (\S\ref{sec:isotropy}).
  \item Reveal a \textbf{smoothness--expressiveness tradeoff} on EquiformerV2/QM9:
    less equivariant activations achieve better task performance, explained by Jacobian
    throughput rather than isotropy (\S\ref{sec:task}, \S\ref{sec:tradeoff}).
  \item Demonstrate that \textbf{Gauss--Legendre quadrature is a drop-in upgrade} for
    EquiformerV2's default Driscoll--Healy grid: GL achieves the same equivariance
    error with $\sim$2$\times$ fewer grid points, both in numerical benchmarks and
    inside trained networks (\S\ref{sec:quadrature}).
  \item Characterize \textbf{per-layer error dynamics} in deep equivariant architectures,
    showing a two-phase accumulation pattern driven by cross-degree coupling
    (\S\ref{sec:perlayer}).
\end{enumerate}

%========================================================================
\section{Background and Definitions}
\label{sec:background}
%========================================================================

\paragraph{S2 Activation pipeline.}
Let $f : S^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be a band-limited function with SH coefficients
$\{c_{\ell m}\}$ for $\ell \le \ell_{\max}$, $m \in \{-\ell,\dots,\ell\}$.
The S2 Activation $A_\sigma$ proceeds in three steps:
\begin{align}
  \text{(1)}\quad f(x_i) &= \textstyle\sum_{\ell=0}^{\ell_{\max}} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}
    c_{\ell m}\, Y_{\ell m}(x_i), \label{eq:fwd} \\
  \text{(2)}\quad g(x_i) &= \sigma\bigl(f(x_i)\bigr), \label{eq:act} \\
  \text{(3)}\quad c'_{\ell m} &= \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i\, g(x_i)\, Y_{\ell m}(x_i),
    \label{eq:inv}
\end{align}
where $\{x_i, w_i\}_{i=1}^N$ is a quadrature rule on $S^2$ with
$\sum_i w_i = 4\pi$.

\paragraph{Angular power spectrum.}
$P(\ell) = \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} |c_{\ell m}|^2$ measures the total energy at degree
$\ell$.

\paragraph{Spectral leakage ratio.}
After applying $\sigma$ to a band-limited input ($\ell \le \ell_{\max}$), the output
generally has energy at all degrees. We measure the leakage via a high-resolution
reference quadrature ($\ell_{\max}^{\mathrm{ref}} = 25$):
\begin{equation}
  R = \frac{\sum_{\ell > \ell_{\max}} P_g(\ell)}{\sum_{\ell \ge 0} P_g(\ell)},
  \label{eq:leakage}
\end{equation}
where $P_g$ is the power spectrum of $g = \sigma \circ f$.

\paragraph{Coefficient error.}
$E_{\mathrm{coeff}} = \|c'_{\mathrm{out}} - c'_{\mathrm{gt}}\| / \|c'_{\mathrm{gt}}\|$,
where $c'_{\mathrm{gt}}$ is obtained with GL quadrature at
$\ell_{\max}^{\mathrm{ref}} = 25$, truncated to $\ell_{\max}$.

\paragraph{Equivariance error.}
For rotation $R \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ with Wigner-$D$ matrix $D(R)$:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{equiv}} = \frac{\|A_\sigma(D(R)\,c) - D(R)\,A_\sigma(c)\|}
  {\|A_\sigma(D(R)\,c)\|},
  \label{eq:equiv}
\end{equation}
averaged over 20 random rotations and 10 random inputs.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
Inputs: $c_{\ell m} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ i.i.d., giving $({\ell_{\max}+1})^2$
coefficients. We test $\ell_{\max} \in \{3, 6, 10\}$ and focus on $\ell_{\max} = 6$
($d = 49$). Activations: $C^\infty$ smooth (Softplus($\beta{=}1$), SiLU, GELU, tanh),
$C^0$ (ReLU, $|\cdot|$), and the parametric family Softplus($\beta$) with
$\beta \in \{1, 3, 10, 30, 100\}$. Quadrature: Gauss--Legendre (GL),
Lebedev, and uniform (equiangular) grids.

\section{Equivariance Error vs.\ Activation Function}
\label{sec:activation}
%========================================================================

We first investigate how the activation choice affects equivariance, holding the
quadrature fixed at GL$_{1\times}$.

\paragraph{Spectral leakage across activations.}
Figure~\ref{fig:leakage} shows the leakage ratio $R$ for each activation at
$\ell_{\max} = 3, 6, 10$. The ranking is consistent:
$C^\infty$ non-saturating activations (Softplus(1): $R{=}0.035$; SiLU: $R{=}0.108$) have
lower leakage than $C^0$ activations (ReLU: $R{=}0.090$; $|\cdot|$: $R{=}0.160$).
The Softplus($\beta$) family interpolates continuously: $R$ increases monotonically with
$\beta$, confirming that leakage is controlled by activation smoothness. Notably, tanh
($R{=}0.094$) has moderate leakage despite being $C^\infty$, because saturation compresses
the output range.

\section{Equivariance Error vs.\ Sampling Strategy}
\label{sec:sampling}
%========================================================================

\subsection{The Truncation--Aliasing Dichotomy}

\paragraph{Coefficient error is sampling-insensitive.}
Figure~\ref{fig:coeff_oversamp} shows $E_{\mathrm{coeff}}$ vs.\ GL oversampling ratio
($1\times$, $2\times$, $3\times$). The curves are nearly flat: increasing quadrature points
from $\sim$100 to $\sim$700 barely changes the output coefficients. This is because the
error is dominated by \emph{truncation}: when $\sigma$ generates energy at $\ell > \ell_{\max}$,
that energy is lost regardless of quadrature accuracy---the output SH basis simply cannot
represent it.

\paragraph{Equivariance error is sampling-sensitive.}
In contrast, Figure~\ref{fig:equiv_oversamp} shows that GL$_{3\times}$ reduces
$E_{\mathrm{equiv}}$ by $10$--$450\times$ compared to GL$_{1\times}$, depending on the
activation (Table~\ref{tab:oversamp}).

\paragraph{Why the dichotomy?}
The key insight is that \emph{truncation error is equivariant but aliasing error is not}.
Truncation is a projection onto the $\ell \le \ell_{\max}$ subspace, which commutes with
SO(3) rotations (each degree $\ell$ transforms independently under the Wigner-$D$ matrices).
It loses information but loses the \emph{same} information regardless of orientation.
Aliasing, however, arises when the quadrature cannot exactly integrate products of high-$\ell$
and low-$\ell$ harmonics. The fold-back pattern depends on the function's orientation
relative to the fixed quadrature grid, so it changes with rotation, breaking equivariance.
Oversampling reduces aliasing by providing enough quadrature points to accurately integrate
these cross-terms. Smoother activations benefit more because they have less high-$\ell$
content to alias.

\section{Jacobian Isotropy Analysis}
\label{sec:isotropy}
%========================================================================

Equivariance is not the only property that matters for downstream tasks: the activation
must also produce diverse nonlinear transformations on the sphere. We quantify this via
the Jacobian of the (discretized) S2 Activation operator.

\paragraph{Definition.}
The S2 Activation $A_\sigma : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ maps input SH coefficients
$c$ to output coefficients $c' = A_\sigma(c)$. Its Jacobian at $c$ is
$J(c) = \partial A_\sigma / \partial c$, a $d{\times}d$ matrix with singular values
$s_1 \ge s_2 \ge \cdots \ge s_d$. We define the \emph{Jacobian isotropy} as the
\emph{effective rank}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{EffRank}(J) = \exp\!\Bigl(-\sum_i p_i \log p_i\Bigr),
  \quad p_i = \frac{s_i^2}{\sum_j s_j^2},
  \label{eq:effrank}
\end{equation}
which counts how many directions in output space the operator locally explores.
$\mathrm{EffRank} = d$ means perfectly isotropic (all $s_i$ equal);
$\mathrm{EffRank} = 1$ means all output variation is along a single direction.

\textbf{Important caveat:} Effective rank measures the \emph{distribution} of singular
values, not their magnitude. A Jacobian $\varepsilon I$ has maximal effective rank but
negligible output variation. We therefore report effective rank alongside the Frobenius
norm $\|J\|_F$ in the supplementary material. For all activations tested,
$\|J\|_F$ is of similar order, so effective rank is a meaningful comparison.

\paragraph{Computation.}
From the S2 Activation forward pass \eqref{eq:fwd}--\eqref{eq:inv}, the Jacobian is
$J = W_Y^\top \,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma'(Y c))\, Y$, where $Y$ is the SH evaluation matrix
and $W_Y$ the weighted inverse matrix. We compute $J$ via autograd and take its SVD.
Results are averaged over 50 random inputs ($c_{\ell m} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$).

\textbf{Note:} The effective rank depends on the specific discretization
(GL$_{1\times}$ here). All isotropy claims in this paper are scoped to this quadrature.

\paragraph{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:effrank} shows the effective rank at $\ell_{\max} = 3, 6, 10$, revealing
a pattern that contradicts naive intuitions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Softplus(1) has the highest effective rank} (33.5/49 at $\ell_{\max}{=}6$),
    because its smooth, non-saturating derivative $\sigma'(f)$ is nonzero and varies
    gradually across the sphere, producing a well-conditioned Jacobian.
  \item \textbf{tanh has low effective rank} (25.6/49), because saturation
    ($\tanh'(f) \to 0$ for large $|f|$) collapses many output directions.
  \item \textbf{$|\cdot|$ has the highest rank} (39.6/49), because
    $|\sigma'(f)| = 1$ a.e., preserving all directions despite being $C^0$.
  \item \textbf{Softplus($\beta$) rank decreases with $\beta$}: as $\beta$ increases
    toward ReLU, the derivative zeros out for $f < 0$, collapsing $\sim$half the
    directions.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:effrank_vs_leak} plots isotropy against leakage, showing the two metrics
are separable. Under GL$_{1\times}$, Softplus(1) and SiLU sit in the low-leakage /
high-isotropy quadrant, while tanh has lower isotropy due to saturation and $|\cdot|$ has
high isotropy but high leakage.

\section{Downstream Task: EquiformerV2 on QM9}
\label{sec:task}
%========================================================================

We test whether the theoretical findings (leakage, equivariance, isotropy) transfer to
a real molecular property-prediction task by swapping the S2 Activation nonlinearity
inside EquiformerV2~\citep{equiformerv2} trained on QM9~\citep{qm9} (U0 target).

\paragraph{Setup.}
We modify EquiformerV2's \texttt{SeparableS2Activation} modules (8 per model) to use each
of 6 activations: SiLU, Softplus($\beta{=}1$), Softplus($\beta{=}10$), tanh, ReLU, and
$|\cdot|$. Architecture: 4 transformer blocks, $\ell_{\max}{=}4$, 64 sphere channels,
5.2M parameters. Training: AdamW, lr $= 2{\times}10^{-4}$, cosine annealing, 50 epochs,
5 seeds per configuration. After training, we measure prediction invariance error
$E_{\mathrm{inv}} = |y(x) - y(R \cdot x)| / |y(x)|$ over random rotations.

\paragraph{What ``default grid'' actually means.}
EquiformerV2's \texttt{SO3\_Grid} delegates to e3nn's
\texttt{ToS2Grid}/\texttt{FromS2Grid}, which uses an \emph{equiangular Driscoll--Healy}
quadrature: latitude nodes $\beta_i = (i + \tfrac{1}{2})\,\pi/N$ with Kostelec--Rockmore
weights, and uniform longitude. With the default $N = 2(\ell_{\max}{+}1)$ latitude points
this is the minimum density at which DH is exact for band-limited functions of degree
$\ell_{\max}$, but it is \emph{not} exact for the products $\sigma(f) \cdot Y_{\ell m}$
that arise in S2 Activation. We therefore study two axes: (i)~the activation choice
above, holding quadrature fixed at the default DH; and (ii)~the quadrature method itself
(\S\ref{sec:quadrature}), holding the activation fixed at SiLU.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:qm9} reports test MAE and prediction invariance error. Surprisingly,
the activation ranking by MAE does \emph{not} follow the equivariance ranking.
Tanh achieves the best MAE (4.42$\pm$0.52~meV) despite having moderate equivariance error,
while Softplus(1)---which has the \emph{lowest} equivariance error (4.9$\times10^{-6}$)---achieves
the \emph{worst} MAE (5.14$\pm$0.52~meV).

\paragraph{Cross-experiment correlations.}
We correlate the theoretical metrics from Experiments A, C, and E with downstream
performance (Figure~\ref{fig:leakage_vs_mae}).
Strikingly, the Spearman correlation between
theoretical equivariance error (Exp~C, GL$_{1\times}$, $\ell_{\max}{=}6$) and downstream
MAE is $\rho = -0.94$ ($p = 0.005$): activations with \emph{higher} theoretical equivariance
error achieve \emph{better} downstream MAE. Spectral leakage shows the same
anti-correlation ($\rho = -0.83$, $p = 0.04$). In contrast, effective rank shows no
significant correlation with MAE ($\rho = +0.26$, $p = 0.62$), nor does the downstream
equivariance error itself ($\rho = -0.37$, $p = 0.47$).

\section{Quadrature Method Inside a Trained Network}
\label{sec:quadrature}
%========================================================================

The numerical analysis of \S\ref{sec:sampling} establishes that Gauss--Legendre needs
roughly half the latitude points of Driscoll--Healy for matched accuracy on band-limited
products. Here we close the loop by replacing EquiformerV2's default DH quadrature with a
custom Gauss--Legendre grid \emph{inside} the trained network and re-running the QM9 sweep.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
We build a drop-in \texttt{CustomSO3Grid} module that exposes the same
\texttt{get\_to\_grid\_mat} / \texttt{get\_from\_grid\_mat} interface as fairchem's
\texttt{SO3\_Grid}, but constructs the matrices by direct evaluation of e3nn real
spherical harmonics on Gauss--Legendre latitude $\times$ uniform longitude nodes. After
model construction we replace each \texttt{SO3\_Grid} buffer in
\texttt{model.backbone.SO3\_grid} with the custom version, leaving every other parameter
identical. We then train SiLU with three quadrature configurations alongside the default,
each with 5 seeds.

\paragraph{Numerical equivariance benchmark.}
Before training, we measure the equivariance error of a single S2 Activation as a
function of grid choice (Table~\ref{tab:quadbench}, $\ell_{\max}{=}6$, $m_{\max}{=}6$):
GL achieves the same equivariance error as DH using approximately half the grid points.
GL with $N_\beta{=}\ell_{\max}{+}1$ and $N_\alpha{=}2\ell_{\max}{+}1$ (the minimal exact
density for products) is competitive with DH at $2\times$ that density.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
At inference time, the S2 Activation cost is dominated by the to/from-grid matrix
multiplications, both linear in the total number of grid points. A switch from DH to GL
at matched equivariance therefore halves these matmul flops. This is a
\emph{drop-in} change with no retraining required: the GL grid can replace the DH grid
in any pretrained EquiformerV2 checkpoint, and the to/from-grid matrices are simply
different orthonormal projections of the same SH coefficients onto a different sphere
sampling. The matching equivariance floors at high resolution (Table~\ref{tab:quadbench})
guarantee that GL converges to the same continuum operator as DH.

%========================================================================
\section{Per-Layer Equivariance Error Accumulation}
\label{sec:perlayer}
%========================================================================

We measure the $\ell{=}0$ invariance error at the output of each of the 4 transformer
blocks in EquiformerV2, revealing a characteristic two-phase pattern
(Table~\ref{tab:perlayer}).

\begin{table}[t]
  \caption{Per-layer $\ell{=}0$ invariance error (mean over 5 seeds, default grid).}
  \label{tab:perlayer}
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc c}
    \toprule
    Activation & L0 & L1 & L2 & L3 & L0$\to$L1 \\
    \midrule
    Softplus(1)    & $1.3{\times}10^{-5}$ & $1.2{\times}10^{-4}$ & $1.8{\times}10^{-4}$ & $2.3{\times}10^{-4}$ & $8.8\times$ \\
    SiLU           & $4.6{\times}10^{-5}$ & $5.7{\times}10^{-4}$ & $5.5{\times}10^{-4}$ & $6.5{\times}10^{-4}$ & $12.4\times$ \\
    tanh           & $7.6{\times}10^{-5}$ & $1.2{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $9.8{\times}10^{-4}$ & $15.7\times$ \\
    Softplus(10)   & $3.3{\times}10^{-4}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.0{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $3.3\times$ \\
    ReLU           & $7.7{\times}10^{-4}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.1{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.5\times$ \\
    $|\cdot|$      & $1.8{\times}10^{-3}$ & $3.3{\times}10^{-3}$ & $2.4{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.6{\times}10^{-3}$ & $1.9\times$ \\
    \midrule
    SiLU ($2\times$)  & $3.8{\times}10^{-7}$ & $3.0{\times}10^{-6}$ & $3.7{\times}10^{-6}$ & $4.2{\times}10^{-6}$ & $7.8\times$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Phase 1: large jump from layer 0 to layer 1.}
The $\ell{=}0$ error increases $1.5$--$15.7\times$ between layers 0 and 1. This reflects
a transition from \emph{indirect} to \emph{direct} contamination. In EquiformerV2's
\texttt{SeparableS2Activation}, the $\ell{=}0$ output is computed from a scalar gating
branch that does not pass through the S2 grid---it has \emph{zero} intrinsic quadrature
error. At layer~0, the $\ell{=}0$ error arises only through indirect coupling:
RMSNorm computes a normalization factor from \emph{all} degrees (including corrupted
$\ell{>}0$ components), and the SO(2) convolution's $m{=}0$ linear layer mixes
$\ell{=}0$ with $\ell{>}0$ coefficients. These are second-order effects.

At layer~1, however, the \emph{input} representation already carries $\ell{>}0$ corruption
from layer~0. The attention mechanism aggregates neighbor features---including their
corrupted $\ell{>}0$ components---to generate new $\ell{=}0$ values via learned linear
projections. This is a \emph{first-order} contamination path, explaining the order-of-magnitude
jump.

\paragraph{Phase 2: plateau at layers 2--3.}
After the initial jump, the error stabilizes or even decreases slightly. Three mechanisms
explain this: (1)~\emph{Residual connections} add a fresh perturbation to the existing
error at each layer, so the relative error $\|e\|/\|x\|$ saturates once the per-layer
increment is comparable to the accumulated error. (2)~\emph{RMSNorm} rescales embeddings
to approximately unit norm at each layer entry, preventing unbounded error growth.
(3)~The spectral character of the error signal reaches a \emph{steady state}: after
several S2 activations, the ``error spectrum'' matches the representation spectrum,
so each layer's corruption adds at a fixed ratio.

\paragraph{The ratio correlates with smoothness.}
Smooth activations (SiLU, tanh, Softplus(1)) have small layer~0 errors but large
L0$\to$L1 ratios (8--16$\times$), because their indirect coupling at layer~0 is weak
but the direct path at layer~1 is equally strong for all activations. Non-smooth
activations (ReLU, $|\cdot|$) already have large layer~0 errors from strong indirect
coupling, so the ratio to layer~1 is smaller (1.5--1.9$\times$).

Notably, $|\cdot|$ is the only activation where the error \emph{decreases} at layers
2--3 (ratios 0.73$\times$ and 0.68$\times$). Since $|x|$ is an even function, it
projects all odd-$\ell$ components to zero, progressively suppressing both the signal
and its error in higher degrees.

%========================================================================
\section{The Smoothness--Expressiveness Tradeoff}
\label{sec:tradeoff}
%========================================================================

The anti-correlation between leakage and downstream MAE (\S\ref{sec:task}) suggests a
fundamental tradeoff: smoother activations preserve equivariance but sacrifice
\emph{expressiveness}---the ability to generate diverse, informative nonlinear
transformations. We formalize this tradeoff by analyzing the Jacobian $J$ of S2 Activation
along multiple axes.

\paragraph{Why effective rank fails as an expressiveness metric.}
Section~\ref{sec:isotropy} showed that the Jacobian effective rank measures how uniformly
singular values are distributed, but ignores their \emph{magnitude}. A Jacobian
$\varepsilon I$ has maximal effective rank but negligible throughput. Indeed, effective rank
has no correlation with downstream MAE ($\rho = +0.26$, $p = 0.62$).

\paragraph{Expressiveness metrics.}
We compute additional Jacobian properties at $\ell_{\max}{=}6$ (50 random inputs):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Nuclear norm} $\|J\|_* = \sum_i s_i$: total signal throughput.
  \item \textbf{Frobenius norm} $\|J\|_F = (\sum_i s_i^2)^{1/2}$: energy throughput.
  \item \textbf{Log-determinant} $\log|\det J| = \sum_i \log s_i$: information-theoretic
    volume preserved by the mapping.
  \item \textbf{Condition number} $\kappa = s_1/s_d$: ratio of strongest to weakest
    direction.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:jacobian} reports these metrics alongside the downstream MAE ranking.

\begin{table}[t]
  \caption{Jacobian metrics ($\ell_{\max}{=}6$, GL$_{1\times}$) and downstream MAE.
  Nuclear norm is the best single predictor of task performance.}
  \label{tab:jacobian}
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
    Activation & $\|J\|_*$ & $\|J\|_F$ & $\log|\det J|$ & EffRank & MAE \\
    \midrule
    tanh       & \textbf{20.0} & \textbf{3.56} & $\mathbf{-66}$ & 25.6 & \textbf{4.42} \\
    $|\cdot|$  & 12.8 & 1.97 & $-75$ & 39.6 & 4.53 \\
    SiLU       & 12.6 & 2.22 & $-94$ & 27.7 & 4.68 \\
    ReLU       & 12.0 & 2.19 & $-177$ & 26.6 & 4.76 \\
    Softplus(10) & 12.0 & 2.17 & $-124$ & 27.1 & 4.79 \\
    Softplus(1) & 11.6 & 1.87 & $-80$ & 33.5 & 5.14 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Nuclear norm predicts task performance.}
The Spearman correlation between nuclear norm and MAE is $\rho = -0.94$ ($p = 0.005$):
higher total throughput predicts lower (better) MAE. Frobenius norm shows a similar trend
($\rho = -0.66$). Log-determinant has a moderate monotonic relationship
($\rho = -0.60$). Condition number and effective rank are uncorrelated with MAE.

\paragraph{Tanh occupies the sweet spot.}
Tanh has the highest nuclear norm (20.0), highest Frobenius norm (3.56), and the least
negative log-determinant ($-66$), while having only moderate leakage and moderate effective
rank. Its saturation at large arguments provides strong nonlinearity (high throughput)
while maintaining a well-conditioned mapping. In contrast, Softplus(1) has the
best-conditioned Jacobian ($\kappa = 17$) and second-highest effective rank, but
its smoothness yields the lowest nuclear norm (11.6)---too little nonlinear signal for the
network to learn from.

\paragraph{The tradeoff is not leakage vs.\ isotropy.}
The earlier analysis (\S\ref{sec:isotropy}) framed the activation choice as a tradeoff
between leakage (equivariance) and isotropy (expressiveness). The downstream results
reveal a different picture: the relevant expressiveness axis is not the \emph{dimensionality}
of the Jacobian (effective rank) but its \emph{magnitude} (nuclear/Frobenius norm).
Spectral leakage correlates with Jacobian magnitude because non-smooth activations
generate stronger nonlinear transformations, which inevitably produce more high-frequency
content. The smoothness--expressiveness tradeoff is thus:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Smoother activations preserve equivariance by suppressing high-frequency generation,
but this same suppression reduces the nonlinear signal throughput that drives learning.}
\end{quote}

%========================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%========================================================================

We identify five axes characterizing S2 Activation and their downstream consequences:

\textbf{1.\ Smoothness $\to$ leakage $\to$ equivariance.}
Smoother activations produce less spectral leakage, which is strongly correlated with
equivariance error. The Softplus($\beta$) family provides continuous parametric control.

\textbf{2.\ Truncation--aliasing dichotomy.}
Coefficient error is truncation-dominated (sampling-insensitive); equivariance error is
aliasing-dominated (sampling-sensitive). This is because truncation commutes with rotation
while aliasing does not.

\textbf{3.\ Quadrature method matters at fixed budget.}
EquiformerV2's default e3nn grid is Driscoll--Healy equiangular, not Gauss--Legendre.
Replacing it with a custom GL grid is a drop-in change requiring no retraining: the GL
grid achieves the same equivariance floor as DH while needing $\sim$2$\times$ fewer
latitude points. This is a pure efficiency improvement with no accuracy cost---and
applies to any pretrained EquiformerV2 checkpoint.

\textbf{4.\ Per-layer error dynamics follow a two-phase pattern.}
Equivariance error jumps $8$--$16\times$ from layer~0 to layer~1 as indirect contamination
(via normalization and SO(2) cross-degree coupling) gives way to direct contamination
(via attention-based message passing with corrupted features). Residual connections and
RMSNorm stabilize the error thereafter.

\textbf{5.\ The smoothness--expressiveness tradeoff.}
Minimizing equivariance error does \emph{not} optimize downstream task performance.
The Spearman correlation between theoretical equivariance error and test MAE is
$\rho = -0.94$: less equivariant activations perform \emph{better}.
The relevant expressiveness metric is not Jacobian isotropy (effective rank) but
Jacobian \emph{throughput} (nuclear norm, $\rho = -0.94$ with MAE). Smooth activations
suppress the high-frequency nonlinear coupling that drives learning, revealing a
fundamental tension between equivariance preservation and expressive power.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
\emph{Inference:} swap the default DH grid for GL with $N_\beta{=}2(\ell_{\max}{+}1)$
and $N_\alpha{=}4\ell_{\max}{+}4$ to halve the to/from-grid matmul cost at matched
equivariance. \emph{Activation choice:} for QM9-style tasks, tanh achieves the best
balance of nonlinear throughput and moderate equivariance. The optimal choice depends
on the task's sensitivity to equivariance vs.\ expressiveness---a tradeoff that merits
further study across architectures and datasets.
